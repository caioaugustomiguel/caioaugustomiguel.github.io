What is Quantitative Easing?

PUBLISHED ON MAR 23, 2020

This topic is definitely a favorite of mine. And today is an exciting day for quantitative easing, so lets get into a short explanation of what it is.

Quantitative easing is a monetary policy tool that involves a central bank going directly to secondary markets and making financial asset purchases. Today, the Fed announced an unlimited quantitative easing program, in which it will purchase treasury securities, corporate and residential mortgage backed securities and, in a twist to some (although I have been saying they would do this since earlier this month), the Fed will purchase bond market ETFs.

The goal of these operations is to keep money flowing in the credit system. During the Great Financial Crisis (GFC, circa ‘08), an alphabet soup of programs were created to maintain the stability of the financial markets, and as a result of its open market operations the Federal Reserve was the proud owner of a shopping mall in 2009.

The Federal Reserve is not the only central bank that utilizes this monetary policy, nor was it the first. The Bank of Japan was the first central bank to engage in QE - all the way back in 2001 - and they still practice it today. Like I mentioned in the previous post, the BoJ is the owner of a large part of Japan’s ETF market. The Fed, with its newly improved alphabet soup, is the late one in the ETF party. Besides the Fed and the BoJ, the European Central Bank, Bank of England, Bank of Ireland, Reserve Bank of Australia, Reserve Bank of New Zealand, and others have also engaged in quantitative easing.

Does quantitative easing work? If the goal of the program is to keep the financial system running, preventing that the credit markets freeze like they did during the GFC - absolutely! If the goal was to bring back inflation - that’s up for debate. But it does work in getting back to business, and that is why central banks are relying on this tool again.
